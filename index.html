Sun-Garden

Trace what blocks your sky — get direct-sun hours for every month of the year.

1 · Where is the garden?

📱 New: AR sun view (beta, phone)

Skip the tracing: stand at the spot, point your phone at the sky, and watch the year's sun paths drawn over your actual trees and fences. Sweep along the arcs and it counts your direct-sun hours on the spot.

🗺 New: Garden sun map (beta)

Sketch your yard from above — house, trees, fences — and get a sun-hours heatmap of the whole garden with a month slider, plus a shadow movie of any day. Deciduous trees go bare in winter here too.

2 · Trace the skyline at each spot

Add a spot for each place you garden — a bed by the fence, the veggie patch, the porch. Each spot gets its own skyline, because the same tree fills a different amount of sky from each viewpoint. Stand there, look around, and drag on the panorama to draw what blocks the sky; drag to the bottom to erase. Use the Deciduous tree brush for trees that drop their leaves — they only block sun during the leaf-on months (drawn in green). Optionally load a panorama photo as a tracing guide — see “How to take the panorama photo” below, then use the alignment fields to position it on the compass.

📷 How to take the panorama photo
  1. Take it with your camera's Pano mode first — this button can't capture a panorama itself; it loads one you've already taken. In the picker, choose Photo Library / Files, not "Take photo" (a single snapshot only covers ~70°).
  2. Stand exactly at the spot — and if the plant is low (a hedge, a bed), hold the phone at the plant's height, since a nearby fence looks completely different from down there.
  3. Before sweeping, note the compass direction you're facing at the start (a compass app helps, or use the sun's position as a reference).
  4. Hold the phone level, keep the horizon across the middle of the frame, and sweep slowly in one smooth pass. Cover the whole arc the sun uses: in the northern hemisphere sweep from northeast through south to northwest. A typical phone pano spans 180–240°.
  5. Load it here — the canvas zooms to the photo so it fills the box for tracing ("Show full sky" switches back). Then set the alignment fields that appear: the direction the left edge faces, how many degrees the sweep covered, and the elevation of the top and bottom edges (a level pano centered on the horizon is roughly −25° to +25°). Nudge until landmarks sit over the right compass letters, then trace — and Undo takes back any stroke.
Too close to trace? Enter measurements instead

For an obstruction right next to the plant — a hedge 2–3 ft from a fence, say — a tape measure beats a panorama. Crucially, enter the height of the plant, not your eye height: a 6 ft fence seen from a 2 ft hedge 2.5 ft away blocks up to 58° of sky; from standing eye height it looks like a harmless 8°. Use any one unit (ft or m) for all lengths. The shape is painted onto this spot's skyline with the selected brush, so a hedge-side maple can go on as a deciduous tree.
